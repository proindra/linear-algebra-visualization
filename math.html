Orthogonal Vectors

The inner product of x(2,1,0) and y(-1,2,0) is zero.
xTy = yTx = 0

  • Zero is the only vector orthogonal to itself.
  • Zero is the only vector orthogonal to every other vector.

Types of Subspaces in ℝ³

A subspace must always contain the origin. In 3D space, there are exactly four types:

  • 0D: The origin alone Z={0}.
  • 1D: Any line through the origin.
  • 2D: Any plane through the origin.
  • 3D: The entire ℝ³ space.

Orthogonal Subspaces

Two subspaces S and T are orthogonal if every x in S is orthogonal to every y in T.
xTy = 0   for all x ∈ S, y ∈ T

  • Z = {0} is orthogonal to all subspaces.
  • In ℝ³, a line can be orthogonal to a plane.
  • In ℝ³, a plane cannot be orthogonal to a plane. → See Tab 4
dim S + dim T ≤ dim V
  • If S and T are merely orthogonal, their dimensions can add up to at most n.
  • If S and T are orthogonal complements, equality holds: dim S + dim T = n.
  • Here n=3. Two orthogonal lines each have dim=1, so 1+1=2 ≤ 3. ✓

"A plane cannot be orthogonal to another plane"

In ℝ³, two 2D planes must intersect along a line. They share the Purple Vector v.

Since v is in both planes and vTv ≠ 0, the planes cannot be orthogonal subspaces.

Orthogonal Complement

The space of all vectors orthogonal to V is the orthogonal complement, written V⊥.

If V is a 1D line, then V⊥ is a full 2D plane. Every vector on the red plane is orthogonal to the blue line.

If S and T are orthogonal complements in ℝn, then:

dim S + dim T = n
  • This is always true — no exceptions.
  • Here n=3, dim V=1, so dim V⊥=2. The line and plane together fill all of ℝ³.
  • Every vector in ℝ³ can be uniquely split into a part in V and a part in V⊥.
